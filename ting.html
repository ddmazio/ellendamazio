The Path of Wayfinding Systems in the UK
UI/UX Design
2025

The Problem

Despite being the most digitally connected generation, Gen Z is experiencing unprecedented levels of loneliness and anxiety around human interaction. Current dating apps and social platforms often heighten performance pressure and diminish authentic connection. What if we could use technology to facilitate real-world moments instead of replacing them—bringing intimacy back to interaction?

The Research

Our research started with a paradox: How can the most connected generation feel the most alone? According to Cigna (2020), 79% of Gen Z reported feeling lonely frequently. McKinsey (2023) adds that 58% have multiple unmet social needs.

From scientific literature to user surveys, we explored: The Polyvagal Theory (Porges), which explains how the vagus nerve governs our sense of emotional safety. The psychological impact of digital overstimulation and the loss of face-to-face rituals. Our own survey (93+ participants) confirmed: people want deeper, more intuitive, and emotionally intelligent ways to connect — starting from the body, not the screen.

User Research

Young adults aged 18–25 expressed difficulty starting conversations, discomfort in social spaces, and fatigue from app superficiality. Most still meet people at school, events, or public spaces — but struggle to initiate or trust these encounters.

They want fewer features, more meaning. Not another feed, but a silent bridge between bodies. That's where ting begins.

Competitive Analysis

We studied platforms like Tinder, Bumble BFF, Hinge, Replika AI, Life360, and Bond Touch. Each has strengths — visual profiles, emotional companionship, location sharing — but they all miss something essential: the body.

Ting reframes the interface of connection as a physical and emotional experience — not just a digital one.

Prototyping

We developed two parallel prototypes:

  • A minimalist mobile interface for onboarding, affective journaling by data, and reflection;
  • A wearable simulation using subtle vibrations to simulate a real-world match.

The mobile UI avoids noise: no swipes, no chats, no pressure — just emotional tracking and consent-based action.

User Testing

In a small testing session with Gen Z participants, feedback was clear:

Ting feels calming, intuitive, and refreshingly human. The absence of visuals was a relief, not a flaw. Participants felt invited rather than overwhelmed.

Critiques included clarifying the meaning of vibrations and refining consent flows. These insights are guiding our next iteration

Visual Identity & Interface

The visual system of ting was designed around softness, warmth, and intuition.

A gradient spectrum inspired by affective states fades from calming blues to energizing yellows and reds. Typography is geometric and round, echoing the product's circular feedback system.

UI elements are minimal, silent, and grounded in emotional clarity.

How ting Works

Once a user signs up, they answer an emotional profile questionnaire to train their personal AI. The wearable continuously reads:

Heart rate (stress or attraction);

Skin temperature (emotional arousal);

Posture (openness or tension);

When two compatible users are within ~10m, the device vibrates softly. If the user taps the notification and agrees to meet, the app opens a shared live map to facilitate a physical encounter. No on-line chat. Just emotional compatibility, consent, and presence.

Final Thoughts

Ting is not another social app. It's a paradigm shift — a return to bodily intuition in a world that has muted it. By prioritizing technology drift isn't preventing, we likely not just a new approach, but a movement step a social a new philosophy.

It doesn't shout. It doesn't sell dopamine. It vibrates quietly — inviting you to listen to yourself, and maybe… to someone else too.
