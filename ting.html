Overview

Ting is a hybrid wearable-app ecosystem designed to restore the intimacy of real-world encounters by translating physiological signals into haptic feedback. Worn on the upper spine, near the vagus nerve, the device vibrates subtly upon proximity to emotionally compatible users, as determined by biometric data processed by an adaptive AI. There are no swipes, no profiles, no images. The body becomes the interface.

Key outcomes

93 survey participants · 57% cited artificial interactions as reason for app abandonment · Prototype validated as "fluid, intuitive, and more human" by test participants aged 18–25.

The Problem

Despite being the most digitally connected generation in history, Gen Z is also the loneliest. According to Cigna (2020), 79% of Gen Z respondents report frequent feelings of loneliness, and 58% claim their social needs remain unmet. McKinsey (2023) corroborates this paradox, linking high digital connectivity to increased anxiety, poor emotional regulation, and a growing sense of inadequacy in face-to-face contexts.

The reliance on visual-first dating apps compounds the problem: swipe mechanics, curated profiles, and image-based judgment reinforce performance anxiety and social fatigue. The challenge was to design a solution that enables users to initiate real-world connection without the pressure of self-presentation, fostering encounters that feel physiologically safe before a word is spoken.

The Goal

To design an embodied interaction tool that allows users to identify emotionally compatible people nearby without screen mediation. Rather than replacing intuition with algorithms, Ting uses bio-data to validate what the body already senses, creating the conditions for spontaneous, low-pressure, real-world connection.

DESIGN PROCESS

Research and Project Definition

The first phase combined quantitative and qualitative methods to map the social and emotional barriers Gen Z faces when building in-person connections. We surveyed 93 participants, mostly aged 18 to 25, and reviewed literature spanning affective computing, neurodesign, and attachment theory.

Three findings shaped the direction of the project:

  • The Loneliness Paradox: 79% of Gen Z respondents report frequent loneliness (Cigna, 2020), yet connectivity has never been higher. The problem is not access — it is the quality and emotional texture of connection.
  • Rejection of Forced Interaction: 57% of survey participants abandon social apps because interactions feel artificial or forced. There is clear demand for organic, lower-stakes ways to meet people.
  • Safety in Physiology: Drawing on Polyvagal Theory (Porges, 1995), we established that the nervous system constantly scans the environment for cues of safety. When those cues are detected — including soft touch or gentle vibration — the ventral vagal complex is activated, enabling social openness and reducing defensive responses. This became the theoretical backbone of Ting.

Competitive benchmarking confirmed a gap in the market. Products like Bond Touch and Neosensory Buzz use vibration to simulate presence, but assume pre-existing relationships. Replika AI and Limitless Pendant advance affective computing, but remain screen-bound and productivity-driven. Traditional dating apps (Tinder, Hinge, Bumble) rely on visual gamification — precisely the dynamics Ting was designed to escape. Life360's spatial interaction model informed our approach to the match tracking feature, but its focus on existing groups left the space for new connection entirely open.

Development and Representation

This phase focused on translating research into a coherent interaction grammar. The wearable was positioned at the upper spine to align with the vagus nerve, grounding the core concept physically: the body senses it first.

On the hardware side, Ting uses photoplethysmography (PPG) for heart rate monitoring, thermal sensors for skin temperature, and an IMU (accelerometer and gyroscope) to assess posture and tension. These inputs feed a local AI model that builds an individualized emotional profile, refined weekly through user feedback. Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) with a 10-meter range enables discreet detection of nearby users, with all data anonymized and stored locally to protect privacy.

The visual identity was built around Paul Ekman's Atlas of Emotions, using a gradient from deep blue (calm and regulation) through coral and orange (emotional activation) to warm yellow (openness and curiosity). The color system is not decorative — it reflects the physiological states the device is designed to navigate.

The interaction model was defined as deliberately minimal: the app functions as a private reflective log and match history, while the wearable operates as the real-time social radar. The design principle was low input, high resonance.

Key outcomes from this phase included the definition of a haptic language — a vibration pattern discreet enough not to demand action, but distinct enough to spark curiosity — and an onboarding flow where users train their personal AI with behavioral data to ensure compatibility matching remains accurate and personal over time.

Key Outcomes

  • Haptic Language Definition: A vibration pattern designed to be discreet enough not to demand action, but distinct enough to spark curiosity — functioning as a whisper rather than a notification.
  • Emotional AI Calibration: An onboarding flow where users train their personal AI model with behavioral data, ensuring compatibility matching remains personal and accurate over time.
  • Visual System: A UI designed to avoid overstimulation, using soft color palettes and gradients calibrated to evoke regulation and calm rather than urgency.

Prototyping and Validation

The final phase brought the Ting ecosystem to high fidelity: detailed UI mockups for the mobile application and 3D visualizations of the wearable device. An informal testing session with participants aged 18 to 25 assessed onboarding flow, interface intuitiveness, and emotional response to a simulated match event.

Feedback validated the core hypothesis. The absence of profile images and swipe mechanics significantly reduced reported performance anxiety. Participants described the interface as fluid, intuitive, and more human. The minimalist layout and color palette were noted as calming and distinctly different from conventional apps. Several participants described the concept as natural and something they would actually want to try.

The Match Tracking feature, inspired by Life360's spatial logic, was refined to allow users to move from a haptic signal to a spontaneous in-person meeting within a structure that felt safe and in their control. Post-interaction prompts — such as “What kind of energy are you bringing today?” — were introduced to support emotional self-awareness and help users engage more intentionally over time.

Key Outcomes

  • User Validation: Participants described the interface as fluid, intuitive, and more human — with particular praise for the absence of visual judgment mechanics.
  • Reflective UX: Post-interaction prompts such as “What kind of energy are you bringing today?” supported emotional self-awareness and helped users engage more intentionally.
  • Physical-Digital Bridge: The prototype successfully demonstrated a seamless transition from physical sensation (haptic vibration) to digital confirmation (app) and back to a physical meeting, closing the loop between body and interface.

TAKEAWAYS

Ting pushed me into territory I hadn't expected: designing not for engagement, but for absence. The most important interaction in this system is the one the app doesn't mediate.

Applying Polyvagal Theory to UX design taught me that the body is an interface in its own right, and that the most effective design sometimes means stepping back entirely to let human connection come forward. It also demanded a rigorous ethical balance: collecting sensitive physiological data while building an experience that feels protective rather than extractive.

This project changed how I think about the role of technology in social contexts. The question stopped being how to make connections faster, and became how to make them safer. That shift in perspective is something I carry into every UX problem I approach now.
