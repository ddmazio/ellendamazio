The Path of Wayfinding Systems in the UK
UI/UX Design
2025

In a world defined by hyperconnectivity, loneliness remains a silent epidemic—particularly among Generation Z. Despite being constantly surrounded by social media, messaging apps, and dating platforms, many young people report feeling more isolated than ever. Our initial research uncovered a troubling paradox: digital environments, rather than bridging gaps, often heighten social anxiety and diminish confidence, prioritizing performance over presence and visual appeal over genuine intimacy.

This insight drove us to explore the intersection of technology and emotional wellbeing. Grounded in behavioral psychology, affective neuroscience, and the principles of emotional design, we investigated the physiological roots of connection—drawing especially from Polyvagal Theory, which links autonomic nervous system states to feelings of safety and openness. Through surveys and interviews with nearly 100 participants, we identified a shared longing: support in initiating real-life interactions, especially for those managing anxiety, social fatigue, or insecurity.

The Solution

Our answer is Ting—a wearable device designed to restore spontaneity and embodied presence in the way we connect. Worn gently at the top of the spine, near the vagus nerve, Ting listens to your body and adapts to your emotional patterns. When someone with compatible emotional signals is nearby, it emits a soft, intuitive vibration—an invitation to connect, without the pressure of images, profiles, or performance-driven interfaces.

The wearable is paired with a mobile app powered by emotional AI, which synthesizes biometric data such as heart rate variability, posture, and skin temperature. Over time, the system learns from your emotional rhythms, offering weekly insights that help users better understand their own social needs and patterns. By transforming the body into a subtle interface, Ting offers a new paradigm for interaction—rooted not in swipes and scripts, but in presence, trust, and embodied resonance. This project was developed within the Interaction Design module of the Communication Design program at FBAUL.
