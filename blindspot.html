Overview

In the age of surveillance capitalism, the most valuable data grows in the shadows, and Blind Spot tries to drag it into the light. The site translates twelve concepts from Shoshana Zuboff’s The Age of Surveillance Capitalism (2018) into a digital experience, making visible the system of extraction, prediction, and behavioural control that quietly runs underneath the internet we use every day.

Its visual identity starts from absence: a black background, red typography, maximum contrast. We built the whole thing in Framer, shaping a navigation structure meant to echo the argument the book itself makes.

In one sentence

A website that makes you perform the same act of unknowing it documents — revealing hidden meaning only when you decide to look.

① The Book

Shoshana Zuboff, 2018

The Age of Surveillance Capitalism is Zuboff’s account of how the digital economy turned human experience into raw material, a commodity to be predicted, packaged, and sold. The book names twelve mechanisms behind this system, from Behavioral Surplus to Big Other.

None of these are abstract. They describe the infrastructure behind every search, every click, every hesitation a screen quietly records. Zuboff argues that most people have never even been given the words for what’s happening to them, and that naming it is the first step toward resisting it.

② The Interaction

To see without being seen.

The central interaction decision mirrors the book’s own argument. The titles of the twelve concepts are visible from the start, in red against black, but their meaning and Zuboff’s excerpts only surface once you hover over them.

The content is there and it’s visible, but it stays opaque until someone decides to look for it — much like the extraction mechanisms the site is describing.

What we tried first

Before landing on this reveal, we tried a multiply blend mode, where the excerpt text already sat on the page and simply changed colour on hover. It felt like a colour trick rather than an act of uncovering, so we dropped it for a version where the text truly isn’t there until you go looking.

Early wireframes also explored adding content with nothing to do with the excerpts — a fake cookie banner, a “be careful” warning — as pure distraction, mimicking the noise real surveillance interfaces bury you in. We left those out of the final site; the hover-reveal carried the argument on its own.

Design decision

The friction is the argument: making the content harder to reach mirrors what it feels like to live inside surveillance capitalism. Usability and conceptual integrity pulled in opposite directions here, and we chose the concept.

③ My Contribution

Building the site in Framer

In this group project, I handled the full technical build in Framer: the hover-reveal component, how the concepts sit on the page, and the interaction logic tying it all together.

□

Hover component

Built once by hand and reused throughout the site — the reveal shows up far more often than the twelve headline concepts, wherever a hidden excerpt needed the same logic.

□

Loose navigation

The concepts sit unaligned on the page instead of in a grid, so reading the site is less about following a menu and more about scrolling and hovering until something catches.

④ Reflections

Blind Spot was the first project that forced me to resolve a real conflict between design clarity and design argument. Almost every interaction principle points toward reducing friction, but here, friction was the whole point.

It also introduced me to critical design as a practice — the idea that a website can make an argument, that how it behaves can carry a claim about the world instead of just displaying information. That idea has stuck with me in every web project since.

Desktop-only, by design

We thought about adapting the interaction for mobile, but decided against it. Hover only really works with a cursor, and any touchscreen version — tap, hold, toggle — would have been a different interaction altogether, not a translation of this one. So the site stays desktop-only, rather than compromise the effect it’s built around.
